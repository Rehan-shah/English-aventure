INTRODUCTION
// LEVEL SELECT

In Tetris, pieces fall one at a time and you do not get to pick them. All you can do is figure out where they go. This quarter felt a lot like that. The assignments came in sequence and I could not always see how they connected: a fairy tale retold as a news article, an essay comparing a rock song to a Brothers Grimm tale, a motif activity that kept sending me back to reread things I thought I had finished. My main claim in this portfolio is that my writing improved when I stopped treating form as a rule to follow and started treating it as a choice to make, and the two showcase pieces are where that shift shows up most clearly. I want you to notice how the revisions changed what I was actually arguing, not just how I said it, and I am using feedback from Rory and specific changes to structure and word choice in both pieces as the evidence for that.

// NEWS ARTICLE REVISION
L-BLOCK · CYAN
Wolf Killed at Brick Residence After Fatal Rampage; Two Dead, Building Codes Under Review
News article revision · By Rehan Shah · Tuesday, April 15, 1890

// REVISED PIECE

FAIRY TALE COUNTY. Two pigs are dead and a third narrowly escaped after a wolf conducted a series of targeted attacks on residential properties in the county's eastern farming district, ending Tuesday night when the animal fell into a pot of boiling water while attempting to enter a brick home through the chimney.

The wolf approached each of the three residences over consecutive days, demanding entry before demolishing the structures and killing the occupants inside. The first two homes, constructed from straw and furze, offered little resistance. The third, built from brick, held.

Authorities say evidence from all three sites suggests premeditation. After failing to breach the brick home by force, the wolf returned on at least three occasions, attempting to draw the resident out using food and social invitations before the fatal chimney attempt Tuesday night.

"He kept coming back," the surviving pig told authorities. "Turnips, apples, a trip to the fair. He wasn't going to stop."

The resident, not publicly identified, said he had placed a pot of boiling water beneath the chimney opening after anticipating the intrusion. The wolf fell in and died at the scene. No other forced entry was found.

The Fairy Tale County Sheriff's Department said no charges would be filed. "Based on the evidence, this appears to be a clear case of self-defense," a spokesperson confirmed.

Reaction among neighbors was divided. Several described the wolf as visibly erratic in recent weeks; others questioned whether the outcome was proportionate.

"Three lives lost over a house," said one resident, who asked not to be named.

County officials announced Monday they would push for updated residential building standards following the structural failures at the straw and furze properties. A public meeting is scheduled Thursday at Fairy Tale County Town Hall. Families of the two deceased could not be reached. A full investigative report is expected within weeks.

REFLECTION · 01

My goal for this piece was to write something that actually sounded like a news article, which meant figuring out what the genre expects and making every choice fit that audience. The biggest change I made was restructuring the opening sentence. In the original version, it started with the wolf, which made him the focus of the whole story. In the revision, it starts with "Two pigs are dead," which puts the victims first and gives the reader the most important information right away. In news writing, the most serious outcome is supposed to go first, so I moved the deaths to the front before introducing who caused them.

I also changed the headline to include "Building Codes Under Review." This makes the story feel like more than just one violent incident. It connects what happened to a community problem, which is what real local journalism does. It also sets up the closing paragraph, where officials call for a public meeting about residential building standards.

Sentence structure was something I paid close attention to in the revision. News writing uses short, direct sentences so facts are easy to follow. In my earlier draft, some sentences were too long and tried to cover too much at once. In the revision, I split information into shorter sentences with one fact each. I also switched to active voice wherever I could. Instead of writing that the structures were demolished, I wrote that the wolf demolished them. Active voice makes sentences feel more immediate and puts responsibility on the right subject.

One smaller change was switching "just" to "proportionate" in the community reaction paragraph. "Just" sounds like a moral judgment, which a news article is not supposed to make. "Proportionate" asks the same question but in more neutral language, which fits the genre better. Every one of these changes came from asking the same question: what would a real reader of a local newspaper expect? Keeping that audience in mind is what pushed me past fixing grammar and into actually rethinking the piece.

SHOWCASE PIECE 02
// COMPARATIVE ESSAY REVISION
O-BLOCK · YELLOW
Sweet Poison: The Motif of Irresistible Entrapment in "Hotel California" and "Hansel and Gretel"
Comparative essay revision · motif analysis across folklore and popular music

// REVISED PIECE

The most dangerous traps do not look like traps. They look like a glowing hotel off a dark desert highway, or a house made of gingerbread deep in the woods, and the reason they work is because they are offering exactly what the victim needs most. The Eagles' 1977 rock song "Hotel California" and the Brothers Grimm fairy tale "Hansel and Gretel" (1812) are both built around this idea, using the motif of temptation leading to entrapment. But they reach very different conclusions about who gets caught, and whether escape is even possible.

The lure begins immediately in both texts, appearing at the exact same structural moment: right when the protagonist thinks they have found salvation. In "Hotel California," a lone traveler spots a lit-up hotel and pulls over. Warm lights, a scent in the air, a woman waving from the doorway. What looks like a perfect resting place slowly reveals itself as something he can never leave. In "Hansel and Gretel," two children abandoned in the forest stumble upon what the Grimm text describes as a cottage "built of gingerbread and covered with cakes, and the windows were of clear sugar" (Grimm, 1812). The moment they begin pulling pieces off the roof, the trap springs, because the witch built that house specifically to catch hungry children. Same motif, same structure, same devastating timing.

What separates the two texts most is how that entrapment is felt, and both works use their own form to make the reader or listener feel it too. "Hotel California" traps its audience alongside the narrator. The chord progression loops without fully resolving; the closing guitar solo stretches on without a clean ending, giving the listener the same dizzy, circular feeling the traveler is experiencing inside the hotel. The music does not just describe the trap. It is the trap. "Hansel and Gretel," written for children, works differently. Its language is simple, its moral clear: the witch is evil, the children are good, and the reader knows this long before the children do. The fairy tale genre also signals from the first page that things will resolve. And they do. When Gretel shoves the witch into the oven, it feels earned and inevitable.

It is worth asking why the two works arrive at such different endings, and the answer lives in context as much as in craft. The Brothers Grimm were collecting and reshaping folk tales in early 19th-century Germany, a time when stories for children carried an explicit moral function: teach the young what the world can do to the innocent, and reassure them that goodness survives. The Eagles were writing in 1970s Los Angeles, deep inside an era of disillusionment. Vietnam, Watergate, the slow burnout of the counterculture. "Hotel California" is widely read as a portrait of excess and self-destruction, where the seductive California dream curdles into something you cannot walk away from. The hopeful fairy tale and the cynical rock song are products of completely different cultural moments, and those moments are written into every line.

The repetition in "Hansel and Gretel," the trail of breadcrumbs, the two abandonments, the mirrored danger and escape, creates a kind of rhythm that does pull the reader in, not unlike the looping chord structure of the song. Both works use formal repetition to reinforce the feeling of being caught in a cycle. But where the fairy tale's repetition leads somewhere (Gretel acts, the cycle breaks), the song's repetition leads nowhere. It spirals. That is the point.

The biggest difference between the two texts, though, is responsibility. In "Hotel California," the night man tells the narrator that the guests are "prisoners here of their own device" (The Eagles, 1977). The traveler chose to stop, chose to stay, kept consuming what the hotel offered. The trap worked because he wanted what it was selling. Hansel and Gretel carry no such weight. They are hungry children who were abandoned by their parents in the woods. Eating a candy house is not a moral failure. It is survival. When the witch locks Hansel in a cage to fatten him up, it is pure cruelty, not consequence.

That distinction is everything. Innocence, in the world of the Brothers Grimm, can be rescued. In the world of the Eagles, desire is its own prison. Both texts use the same motif to make very different arguments about the world. One says that traps can find people who did nothing wrong, and that courage is enough to break free. The other says that some traps are built from a person's own wanting, and the most seductive ones are the hardest to leave. The motif looks different depending on who is walking into it and why. In one story, the door swings open. In the other, you can check out any time you like, but you can never leave.

The Eagles. "Hotel California." Hotel California, Asylum Records, 1977.

Grimm, Jacob, and Wilhelm Grimm. "Hansel and Gretel." Grimm's Fairy Tales, 1812. Translated by Edgar Taylor and Marian Edwardes, Project Gutenberg, 2009.

REFLECTION · 02

My goal for this essay was to make a real argument about how two very different texts use the same motif, and to go deeper than just listing what they have in common. When I first wrote this essay, I was just trying to hit the five-paragraph format and get through it. Introduction, three body paragraphs, conclusion, done. Looking back at it now, I can see how much that structure was actually limiting what I was saying. The ideas were there, but they were getting forced into boxes that did not really fit them, and the writing felt kind of mechanical because of it.

For this revision, the biggest thing I changed was letting the essay breathe more. Instead of locking every idea into its own paragraph with a clean transition, I tried to let the analysis move the way an actual argument moves, building on itself, circling back, making connections across sections instead of keeping everything neatly separated. The section on historical context was something Rory flagged, and she was right. I was making claims about what these works "meant" without ever stopping to ask why they were made in the first place. Adding that context, the Grimm Brothers writing moral tales for 19th-century children versus the Eagles capturing 1970s disillusionment, actually changed how I understood the whole comparison. It was not just two stories using the same motif. It was two completely different cultural moments producing two completely different ideas about whether people can be saved.

I also worked through the comment about repetition and whether "Hansel and Gretel" creates an entrancing effect the way the song does. I think it does, but the key difference is that the fairy tale's repetition actually leads somewhere. The cycle breaks when Gretel acts. The song's repetition just spirals. Working that out helped me land on what ended up being the actual argument of the essay: that "the motif looks different depending on who is walking into it and why." This revision still is not perfect, but it feels a lot more like something I actually wrote instead of something I assembled.

FINAL REFLECTION
// Rehan Shah · Folklore Genres · ENGL 131
"Stories reflect their time; they cannot help but do so."
- Rory, class discussion

Before this class I thought writing worked a specific way: you had something to say, you organized it, you typed it out, and if it came out messy that meant something was wrong with you specifically. The Lamott reading was the first thing that broke that down. She describes sitting down to write a food review and producing a lead paragraph a full page long, with imaginary critics on her shoulders making fun of everything she typed. I recognized that feeling immediately. I just did not know that writers who actually get published still felt it too. She calls believing in clean first drafts "the fantasy of the uninitiated," which was uncomfortable to read because that was exactly my fantasy. What made it land was that she proved her argument by the way she was writing, loose and honest and like a real person talking, which is something I had not thought of as a deliberate choice before.

The nonfiction unit pushed that further. I assumed most texts would follow a similar academic style, which turned out to be completely wrong. Anzaldúa wrote in a braided essay moving between languages and identities. Halberstam opened "Low Theory" with a SpongeBob and Mr. Krabs dialogue to argue about capitalism, which I did not know what to do with at first but which worked really well once I got past the strangeness. Cohen built a whole piece around a poem, pulling from it out of order, and used photographs. Every writer found a different form, and all of them worked. That was one of the more useful things I took away: form is something you choose based on what you are trying to say, not a rule you follow because that is how writing goes.

The Puss in Boots comparison is probably what I think about most when I try to explain what this class did for me. The Perrault version felt cold and transactional, more like a guide to social climbing than a fairy tale, which makes sense when you think about the moment it came from. The DreamWorks version is almost the opposite: Puss wants somewhere to belong, not wealth or power, which fits with what post-9/11 America seemed to need from its stories. The Garfield version I initially thought was just badly made, but the more I sat with it the more it started to look like a parody of Perrault's earnestness. Rory kept saying stories reflect their time and cannot help but do so, and I did not really feel that until I put all three versions side by side and watched the same basic plot carry completely different meanings.

The bigger shift is less about specific things I learned and more about a different starting assumption. Writing is supposed to be messy at first. Form is a choice, not a given. The same story can mean completely different things depending on when it was made. And changing your mind is not a problem: it is how the thinking gets somewhere. None of that was obvious to me in September. I think those shifts will matter in other classes too, not just English ones. Knowing that form is a choice means I can ask what a piece of writing needs to do before I start, and that is a useful question in pretty much any class that asks you to write.

# Final Reflection
**Rehan Shah**
**Folklore Genres · ENGL 131**

> "Stories reflect their time; they cannot help but do so."
> - Rory, class discussion

Before this class I thought writing worked a specific way: you had something to say, you organized it, you typed it out, and if what came out was messy or uncertain that meant something was wrong with you specifically. The Lamott reading was the first thing that started to break that down, mostly because she just says "shitty" in the title and keeps going like it is nothing, and I paid more attention to that essay than I usually pay to assigned reading. She describes sitting down to write a food review and producing a lead paragraph a full page long, with imaginary critics on her shoulders making fun of everything she typed. I recognized that feeling immediately. I just did not know that writers who actually get published still felt it too. She calls believing in clean first drafts "the fantasy of the uninitiated," which was uncomfortable to read because that was exactly my fantasy, and I think what made it land was that she was proving her argument by the way she was writing, loose and honest and like a real person talking, which is something I had not really thought of as a choice a writer could make on purpose.

The nonfiction unit pushed that further in a different direction. I went in assuming most of the texts would follow roughly the same academic style, which turned out to be completely wrong. Anzaldúa wrote in a braided essay moving between languages and identities. Halberstam opened "Low Theory" with a dialogue between SpongeBob and Mr. Krabs to make an argument about capitalism, which I did not know what to do with at first but which worked really well once I got past the strangeness, because the cartoon framing made the argument easier to follow than a more traditional structure would have. Cohen built a whole piece around a poem, pulling from it out of order, and used photographs to help the reader see what he was describing. Every writer found a completely different form, and all of them worked, which was one of the more useful things I took away from the quarter: form is something you choose based on what you are trying to say, not a set of rules you follow because that is how writing is supposed to go. I think that connects to what the class kept pushing toward in terms of being able to write for different audiences and purposes, because once you see that the form can shift depending on what the argument needs, you start thinking about writing as a set of actual decisions rather than a checklist.

The motif activity was a smaller moment but it stuck with me. I kept writing motifs that were too broad, basically just summarizing plot, and the shift of narrowing them down and getting specific about what the actual repeating element was felt like learning a different way to look at a story. That carried over into how I read everything after, especially the Puss in Boots comparison, because I was paying closer attention to what was actually repeating across versions rather than just what the plot was doing.

That comparison is probably what I think about most when I try to explain what this class actually did for me. The Perrault version felt cold and transactional in a way I did not expect going in, because the cat builds a fake identity, the ogre gets eaten, and nobody reflects on any of it, and the morals at the end read less like lessons and more like observations about how power works if you are paying attention, which makes more sense when you think about the moment Perrault was writing in, when the idea of reinventing yourself through cleverness rather than inherited status was genuinely new. The DreamWorks version is almost the opposite, where Puss is not interested in wealth or power and is mostly motivated by wanting somewhere to belong, which fits with what post-9/11 America seemed to need from its stories. The Garfield version I initially just thought was badly made, because Puss is lazy and apathetic and nothing really resolves, but the more I sat with it the more it started to look like a parody of Perrault's earnestness, which is a harder thing to pull off than just making a straightforward version of the story. Rory kept saying in class that stories reflect their time and cannot help but do so, and I did not really feel that until I put all three versions side by side and watched the same basic plot carry completely different meanings depending on when and why it was made.

I think the bigger shift is less about specific things I learned and more about a different starting assumption. Writing is supposed to be messy at first. Form is something you choose, not something you follow. The same story can mean completely different things depending on the moment it comes out of. And being wrong about something, or changing your mind, is not a problem: it is actually how the thinking gets somewhere useful. None of that was obvious to me in September, and I do not think any of it came from a single assignment but from the way all of it kept building on the same questions across the quarter.
